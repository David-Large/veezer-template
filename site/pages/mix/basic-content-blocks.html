A master armoursmith first considers who will wear the armour: a knight sworn to an ancient order, a desert wanderer, or a dragon-hunter marked by old battles. Culture, environment, and personal history shape every choice, from the silhouette of the helm to the symbols etched along the breastplate. Armour in fantasy is never just protection—it is identity made visible. Materials are where imagination truly takes hold. Mythril threads, dragonbone plates, enchanted leather, or rune-tempered iron each suggest different strengths and limitations. Broad shoulders might signal power, spiked edges may warn of danger, and flowing lines can suggest speed or grace. These visual cues help the audience immediately understand the wearer’s role, whether they are a noble defender, a ruthless warlord, or something far less human. Function still matters, even in the most magical designs. Articulated joints, overlapping plates, and reinforced weak points ground the armour in reality. Designers often borrow from historical armour—*Roman lorica, samurai lamellar, or medieval plate*—and then push those forms beyond history with fantasy materials and impossible craftsmanship. The final step is unifying the armour with the world it belongs to. Colours, engravings, and insignia connect it to kingdoms, guilds, or ancient prophecies.
